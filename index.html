CAPTA-Vated Audience: Utilizing GIS to Analyze Radio Spectrum Enforcement

IN PROGRESS

Phase I - Spectrum Specific Interference and Enforcement

More About This Project

The United States is experiencing spectrum scarcity, or so most research and initiatives would have the community believe. This argument has been the premise for several initiatives on how to best utilize and allocate radio spectrum. However, with the advent of the Internet of Things and 5G services, it has become important to take a look at the governing policy and regulatory efforts in order to develop measures on how to deal with enforcement â€“ specifically automated enforcement- for spectrum interference. My research for this project investigates the enforcement of commercial spectrum and how the Federal Communication's Enforcement Bureau adjudicates interference issues within radio frequency bands. My hypothesis is that radio spectrum interference is an important and serious issue that needs to be alleviated before emerging technologies enter the current spectrum environment. Moreover, it is possible that there is an enforcement disparity in which "would be offenders" are not necessarily dissuaded from interfering with radio spectrum. Through my research, I hope to gain a better understanding of the enforcement measures and process in order to develop an automated enforcement structure that would best encapsulate policy/ regulator needs and requirements for fair and consistent enforcement of radio spectrum.

Although my research will not include the federal spectrum element (due to lack of resources available and time), what I am working on will be salient to the ongoing discussions on how to tackle enforcement (namely, automated enforcement) within shared spectrum environments. Additionally, since the FCC is no longer maintaining their spectrum database, my research will aid in realizing the typical hot spots for interference (and recurring types of interference). Moreover, by completing this project, other researchers may be able to gain a better idea of who the repeat offenders within shared spectrum spaces. The benefits of this research are that it is current and provides a guide (and hopefully if developed further) a roadmap of how we can begin to automate spectrum enforcement.
